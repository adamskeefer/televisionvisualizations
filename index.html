Television Over Time

The television has long been a staple of the American household, a centerpiece of the average living room. The device became a new way to broadcast information, as well as sporting events. But its biggest innovation may have come from a new art form it ushered in, the Television series. Since the very first TV show was released, we have seen the medium undergo drastic changes. From the 1983 M*A*S*H series finale being the most watched American broadcast outside the Super Bowl, to The Sopranos paving the way for Premium Cable and the Golden Age of Television, to today where most television consumption is done through streaming services.

This project aims to visualize statistics about shows released throughout 6 eras of television: The Golden Age (1947-1960), The 1960s, The 70s, The 80s, The 90s, and the Second Golden Age (2000-Present). The project will show how the structure of shows has changed over time (i.e. Episode length, season length, series length), as well as how public perception has changed (i.e. Show popularity and show rating).

Methodology

Data Collection: In this project I used a dataset containing the 150,000 television shows listed on TMDB. Since that amount of shows would have been a nightmare to visualize, I ran an R script to filter through the data and pull out only relevant shows. To establish relevancy, I used the field id "vote_count" which indicated how many people had logged a review of the show. Since the internet is knew, this is not the best way to indicate relevancy of old shows and thus I set the threshold to different values depending on the era. I wanted to demonstrate how many more shows we have today compared to back then, but I also wanted to ensure I had a sizable subset for the old eras.

The Popularity Index: In the visualizations below, you will see that I used something called the Popularity Index. I calculated this myself using a combination of the "popularity" field of the dataset and the "vote_count" field. First, I averaged the two values together, then, I normalized the highest indexed show to 100 for the early eras. The normalization however did not work for recent eras because of some shows being monumentally more popular than the rest from that era *coughcough "The Simpsons" and "Friends". Thus, I presented these on a logarithmic scale so that the viewer could still hover over individual data points.

Foreign Shows: After much debate I decided to included television shows produced in foreign countries. While these shows don't always follow American storytelling structure, television as a storytelling device has American influences that are simply unavoidable and I thought it would be interesting to show how even Foreign shows can share similarities with American ones. Plus, with television becoming more and more accessible, many Americans watch foreign shows and I felt I would be doing a disservice by intentionally excluding shows that a lot of people watch.
